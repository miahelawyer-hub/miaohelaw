Support is one of the most contested issues in any divorce. Understanding how Ontario law approaches both [child support and spousal support](/en/family-law/support) will help you negotiate from a position of knowledge.

---

## Child Support

### Is Child Support Mandatory?

Yes. [Child support](/en/family-law/support) in Ontario is not optional. Under the federal *Divorce Act* and Ontario's *Family Law Act*, a parent has a legal obligation to support their children financially. The amount is determined by the **[Federal Child Support Guidelines](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/regulations/sor-97-175/index.html)**.

### How Is It Calculated?

The guideline amount depends on:

1. **The paying parent's gross annual income**
2. **The number of children**
3. **The province of residence** of the paying parent

The Guidelines provide a fixed [table amount](/en/family-law/support) based on these variables. Both parties cannot agree to pay *less* than the table amount — any agreement to do so is not enforceable.

### Special or Extraordinary Expenses

In addition to the table amount, parents may also share **[Section 7 expenses](/en/family-law/support)** — special or extraordinary expenses for the child, such as:

- Childcare costs
- Medical and dental expenses not covered by insurance
- Extracurricular activities
- Post-secondary education

These are typically shared proportionally based on each parent's income.

### What If the Paying Parent's Income Changes?

[Child support](/en/family-law/support) can be varied if there is a material change in circumstances — such as a significant change in income. Either party can bring an application to vary the [existing child support order](/en/family-law/support).

## Spousal Support

### Is Spousal Support Automatic?

No. Unlike [child support](/en/family-law/support), [spousal support](/en/family-law/support) is not automatic. There must be an entitlement — based on compensatory, non-compensatory, or contractual grounds — before any amount is determined.

### Grounds for Spousal Support

- **Compensatory:** One spouse gave up career opportunities or education during the marriage to support the family or the other spouse's career.
- **Non-compensatory:** One spouse is in financial need following the [breakdown of the marriage](/en/family-law/domestic-contracts#separation).
- **Contractual:** A [domestic contract](/en/family-law/domestic-contracts) (such as a [marriage contract](/en/family-law/domestic-contracts)) provides for support.

### How Is It Calculated?

The **[Spousal Support Advisory Guidelines (SSAG)](https://www.justice.gc.ca/eng/fl-df/spousal-epoux/ssag-ldfpae.html)** provide a range for both the amount and duration of spousal support. Unlike child support, these are advisory — not mandatory — and courts have significant discretion.

Key factors include:

- Length of the marriage
- Each spouse's income and earning capacity
- The roles each spouse played during the marriage
- Sacrifices made to support the other spouse's career

### How Long Does It Last?

Duration varies widely. For shorter marriages, support may be time-limited. For long marriages — particularly where one spouse was out of the workforce for a significant period — support may be indefinite.

## Practical Advice

Support disputes are often the most emotionally charged part of a divorce. Before agreeing to any [support arrangement](/en/family-law/domestic-contracts#separation), make sure you understand:

- Whether [child support](/en/family-law/support) is being calculated at the correct guideline amount
- Whether you have an entitlement to [spousal support](/en/family-law/support)
- What range the [SSAG](https://www.justice.gc.ca/eng/fl-df/spousal-epoux/ssag-ldfpae.html) suggests for your situation
- Whether a time-limited or indefinite arrangement makes more sense

Getting these numbers right matters — not just now, but for years to come.
